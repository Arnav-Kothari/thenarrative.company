Three honest notes

If I could tell you three true things about your X, these are them.

Not the polished deck. The version I'd give you if we were sitting at dinner. Every number here is from the Jan-Mar 2026 peer analysis, and every claim is something I'd defend to your face. Read it in that spirit.

Note 01

You post too much. That's the single biggest thing holding your reach back.

You posted 4.2 times a week. Pichai posted 2.8. Nadella posted 2.2. Your median reach was 51K. Pichai's was 347K. Nadella's was 682K. You are posting twice as often as the people beating you and pulling 7x to 13x less reach for the effort.

This is not an audience problem. You have more followers than most people in the set. It's a frequency problem. Every extra post dilutes the signal of the one before it and trains both the algorithm and your audience to scroll past you. The posts are competing with each other instead of compounding.

This is the one lever where the data is unambiguous. Nothing else matters as much.

Do this
Cut from 4.2 to 2 posts a week. Protect each one like it's the only shot this week.
Note 02

Your engagement rate is last in the peer set. That's a writing problem, not a reach problem.

Your engagement rate was 0.51%. Last in the set. Bill McDermott, who posts twice a quarter and pulls 7K median views, runs at 1.18%. More than double yours.

What that actually means: when people do see your posts, they engage with them less than they do with anyone else in the peer group. You can't fix that by getting more reach. The problem isn't who's seeing the post. The problem is what the post is.

The pattern is visible in the data. The posts that flatlined were congratulations, product news, and supportive retweets. The posts that spiked - "Impressive." on Figure 03, the SaaSpocalypse moment, your replies to founders in your mentions - had a point of view, a stake, a Marc-ism. People engage when they feel a specific person behind the keyboard, not a corporate account in a polo.

Do this
Before you publish, ask: is there a take in here that only I can make? If no, it goes to @salesforce.
Note 03

You're running your personal X like it's Salesforce's second PR channel. It shouldn't be.

Patrick Collison posts roughly every other week. 0.5 posts a week. He has 78 high-value commenters in this window - more than anyone else in the set by a factor of 2.6x. Your high-value commenter count is 30. Solid, but nowhere near.

The reason isn't his network. It's what he posts about. When Collison shows up, he says things most CEOs wouldn't bother saying: a question he's wrestling with, a book that rearranged his thinking, a pattern he's noticing in the economy. Those posts earn the founders, operators, and writers in the replies - and those people bring their friends.

You are the most recognizable CEO in SaaS. The rarest thing on X right now is a forty-year operator saying what they actually think about the future, the market, AI, the next wave, anything. That is a niche only you can fill. Every post you burn on a product launch or a customer shoutout is a post that isn't doing that work, and those launches and shoutouts have a perfectly good home on the Salesforce handle.

Do this
Reserve the Benioff account for Benioff takes. Product wins, customer love, event promos all move to @salesforce.

These three notes compound. Post less, say something only you can say, and stop letting your personal account do corporate work. Do those three things and the reach, the engagement, and the right audience all move in the same direction. That's the whole play.
